Theoretical Orientation

1. Academic Vision: Mathematical and Sociological Inquiry into Social Structures and Networks My scholarly inquiry has always been oriented toward the essence of sociology. Since my master's program, I have sustained a deep interest in social networks, social structures, and the processes through which knowledge and information diffuse, rigorously exploring the relevant theories and methodologies. In particular, after encountering former Yonsei University President Kim Yong-hak's Social Network Theory during my graduate studies, I became profoundly drawn to the work of mathematically modeling and understanding social relationships and structures. More recently, I have been developing conceptual models concerning network topology and centrality — for instance, an idea that connects the mathematical concept of a limit to the ideal network structure and the form of the self, by analogy with how a polygon approaches a circle as the number of its sides increases, causing its centroid to converge toward the center.

2. Methodological Competence: Data Analytic Capacity Bridging Theory and Technology I believe that the depth of research is grounded in rigorous methodology. During my master's program I extensively studied research methods literature and applied SPSS and Stata to panel data analysis in actual research projects. Beyond that, I pursued advanced study of Python, SQL, and Swift to build contemporary data processing capabilities. By strengthening my command of technical analytical tools, I have laid the groundwork to extend sociological inquiry into more precise, data-driven research.

3. Sustained and Consistent Preparation for Doctoral Study Pursuing a doctorate is not a momentary decision but a lifelong plan I have carefully refined over many years. As far back as my master's preliminary examination, I had doctoral study in view — to the extent that I deliberately coordinated my contract employment schedule and resignation timing so as to immerse myself in research. Although external circumstances occasionally shifted the timeline, I never ceased developing my knowledge of sociological theory and methodology, and my commitment to research has remained unwavering throughout.

4. Academic Path and Growth: Toward the Essence of Human Beings and the Structure of Knowledge My academic journey has been marked by meaningful turning points. When I first entered graduate school, I did so on the basis of practical experience in elder welfare; I subsequently transferred to the Sociology and Cultural Studies track in order to pursue statistical methodology more deeply, ultimately earning a master's degree in sociology — a decision I remain genuinely proud of. For the 2026 fall admissions cycle, I applied to the doctoral program in Artificial Intelligence at Korea University and was selected as an interview candidate. Through discussions with my prospective advisor, I confirmed that my interdisciplinary interests were well-suited to the laboratory's research agenda, which spans brain engineering and cognitive science. Nevertheless, I ultimately chose not to sit for the final interview due to administrative constraints on changing majors. That experience proved to be a significant turning point. It clarified that my deeper passion lies not in technology per se, but in exploring the structural diffusion of human knowledge and society mediated by technology — and through that realization, I arrived at a much clearer sense of where my research belongs: at the intersection of digital humanities informatics and advanced sociology.

Just as society advances through continuous transformation, my own academic path has been a positive evolution — one that has progressively sharpened the focus of my research. Equipped with powerful tools in data analysis (Python, SQL, Stata), I am committed to becoming a researcher who elucidates the dynamics of social structures and relationships through mathematical and sociological inquiry.
